## Disqualifying defects (invariant violations, if any — cite the invariant)
None rise to disqualifying. One buyer-relevant concern, not a DQ: **instrument** commits the whole page to an off-black ground with monospace-labelled term rows — read on a laptop between meetings (and worse on a bright phone in a hallway) it is the least eye-comfortable of the three, and the "000% / 052% / 100%" scroll counter plus the giant ARCHON wordmark read as designer-for-designers flourishes that a non-technical partner will glance past rather than trust more for.

## Verdict (ranked, 3 sentences max each)
1. **artifact** — It answers the only question I actually have ("what exactly am I buying?") in the headline itself and then *shows* me the workflow unit as a labelled figure, so the AI stops being abstract and starts looking like a thing I could point my office manager at. The "what it does" list is plain English a non-lawyer partner reads in five seconds — intake reads itself, drafts are waiting, clean file trail — and "the first step costs twenty minutes and nothing else" removes the last excuse not to book. Warmest-to-read background, most prominent and most-repeated CTA, zero hype smell: this is the one I'd book from.
2. **ledger** — This is the only variant that feels like it was made for *my* world: it reads like a written legal memo, it puts an anonymized law-firm engagement front and centre, and "this page is typeset like one, so you can judge the work before the call" is a genuinely disarming trust move that demonstrates competence instead of claiming it. Highest pure premium/credibility feel and beautifully comfortable to read on both laptop and phone. It loses first place only because its clarity is a half-step softer — "your assessment has already begun" is clever but I have to work slightly harder than with artifact to know precisely what lands on my desk.
3. **instrument** — The "Engagement Terms" table (Call 20 min · Assessment 48h written · Build 2–4wk · Price fixed, agreed first · Run hosted) is the single best "what am I committing to" artifact across all three and I respect its confidence ("if it takes longer, that's our problem"). But the dark machinery aesthetic and monospace labelling read as *for technical buyers*, which is not me, and it's the most tiring to read in a stolen two minutes between meetings. Strong substance, wrong temperature for a non-technical partner — it earns a call, but it's the one I'd hesitate over.
